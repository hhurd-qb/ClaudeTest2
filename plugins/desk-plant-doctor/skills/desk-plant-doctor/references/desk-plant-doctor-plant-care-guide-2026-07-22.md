# Office Desk Plant Care Guide

A quick-reference diagnostic guide for the most common office/desk plants. Match the plant, then match the symptom — the same symptom can mean opposite things depending on the plant.

## Quick ID (if they don't know the plant name)

* **Heart-shaped or arrow-shaped trailing vine leaves, often variegated** → Pothos
* **Long, upright, stiff, sword-like leaves, sometimes striped yellow/green** → Snake Plant (Sansevieria)
* **Glossy, dark green, oval leaves on a sturdy upright plant** → ZZ Plant
* **Delicate feathery/lacy fronds** → Peace Lily or a fern
* **White flower spikes/blooms with dark green leaves, droops dramatically when thirsty** → Peace Lily
* **Small, chubby, water-storing leaves** → Succulent
* **Large, split or perforated leaves** → Monstera / Philodendron

---

## Pothos

* **Yellow leaves, especially lower/older leaves**: Overwatering. Let soil dry out fully between waterings — pothos tolerates neglect far better than excess water.
* **Brown, crispy leaf edges**: Underwatering or low humidity (common near AC vents). Water more consistently; misting helps in dry office air.
* **Leggy vines with leaves far apart, pale color**: Not enough light. Move closer to a window or under stronger office lighting; pothos tolerates low light but "tolerates" isn't "thrives."
* **No new growth for months**: Needs fertilizer (even office plants benefit from occasional dilute fertilizer) or is rootbound — check if roots are circling the pot.

## Snake Plant (Sansevieria)

* **Yellow or mushy leaves at the base**: Overwatering — the #1 killer of snake plants. It wants to dry out completely between waterings, often every 2-3 weeks.
* **Wrinkled, slightly droopy leaves**: Underwatering, but this is much rarer than overwatering — snake plants are extremely drought-tolerant.
* **Brown tips**: Usually fluoride/chemicals in tap water or low humidity — not urgent, mostly cosmetic.
* **Leaning or falling over**: Normal for tall snake plants top-heavy for their pot; not usually a health issue, just needs a heavier pot or support.

## ZZ Plant

* **Yellow leaves**: Almost always overwatering. ZZ plants store water in underground rhizomes and want to dry out completely — water only every 2-4 weeks depending on light.
* **Stems going soft/mushy at the base**: Root rot from overwatering — may need to cut back affected stems and reduce watering drastically.
* **Slow or no growth**: This is often completely normal — ZZ plants are naturally slow growers, especially in office lighting. Not necessarily a problem to fix.

## Peace Lily

* **Dramatic drooping, whole plant looks collapsed**: Classic underwatering — peace lilies are famous for this "SOS drama" and usually perk back up within hours of watering.
* **Yellow leaves despite regular watering**: Could be overwatering (check drainage) or too much direct sun — peace lilies prefer indirect light.
* **Brown leaf tips**: Tap water sensitivity (fluoride/chlorine) or low humidity — let tap water sit out 24 hours before using, or switch to filtered water.
* **No white blooms**: Not enough light. Peace lilies need bright indirect light to flower, even though they tolerate lower light to just survive.

## Succulents

* **Mushy, translucent, or discolored leaves**: Overwatering/root rot — the most common succulent killer in offices with well-meaning coworkers watering it "just in case." Let soil dry out completely, water sparingly.
* **Shriveled, wrinkled leaves**: Underwatering — less common in offices, but happens if it's fully neglected for months.
* **Stretching upward with wide gaps between leaves ("etiolation")**: Not enough light. Office succulents almost always need more light than they're getting — a sunny windowsill, not a desk under fluorescent lights.

## Monstera / Philodendron

* **Yellow leaves**: Overwatering, same pattern as most plants on this list — check that the pot drains well and isn't sitting in standing water.
* **Small leaves with few or no splits/holes**: Not enough light or not mature enough yet — splits ("fenestration") develop more with age and brighter light.
* **Brown crispy edges**: Low humidity or underwatering — office AC is usually the culprit; occasional misting helps.
* **Leaning or flopping over**: Needs a moss pole or stake — these are naturally climbing plants and get top-heavy without support.

## General office-plant truths

* **The single most common cause of office plant death is overwatering**, not underwatering — despite what people assume from neglect. Desk plants get "loved to death" more often than forgotten to death.
* **Standing water in a saucer or decorative pot with no drainage hole** is a very common hidden cause of root rot — always check for this first if a plant is declining despite a "reasonable" watering routine.
* **Office lighting is almost always dimmer than people think.** Fluorescent office light is much weaker than a sunny window; when in doubt, the fix is often "more light," not more water or fertilizer.
